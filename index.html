01 The Crisis

A forest stripped to bone.

The Mediterranean Sea hosts a remarkable diversity of coral species that most people — including many divers — do not associate with temperate waters. Gorgonians form dense, fan-shaped colonies on rocky walls, creating three-dimensional habitats that shelter hundreds of other species. They are habitat engineers: remove them, and the wall becomes a parking lot where there was once a neighborhood.

Marine heat waves have become more frequent and more intense in the Ligurian Sea over the past two decades. When water temperatures exceed the thermal tolerance of these species, the result is mass mortality — living tissue dies, sloughs off, and colonies that took decades to grow are reduced to bare skeletons within a single summer.

The gorgonians were denuded. What remained were bare skeletons — still standing in the shape of fans, but holding nothing alive.

Anchoring, lost fishing lines, and careless diver contact compound the damage. The fragments that result — a broken branch of Eunicella, a detached piece of red coral — would, without intervention, fall to the sediment and perish. Coral Care was built to rescue what can still be saved.

02 The Project

Three partners. One mission.

The Coral Care Project is a collaboration between three organizations, each bringing something the others could not provide alone. Conservation projects cannot succeed if they are driven by science alone, by institutions alone, or by passionate divers alone. You need all three.

Operations

Reef Alert Network

The nonprofit conservation diving organization that provides the operational framework, the trained volunteer divers, and the capacity to execute complex underwater operations.

Institution

Portofino MPA Consortium

One of Italy's oldest marine protected areas. Provides institutional backing, site access, and regulatory oversight — without which this work would not be possible.

Science

UNIVPM

The Polytechnic University of Marche provides the scientific foundation: the protocols, the research expertise, and the specialized resin tested for soft-coral attachment.

03 The Protocol

From fragment to brick.

A critical principle guides the entire process: minimize stress on the coral. Everything that can be done underwater, is done underwater. In less than an hour, rescued fragments are already placed on the nursery structures.

01

Collection

Viable fragments — with living tissue and active polyps — are carefully detached or retrieved along the walls of the MPA, typically between 25 and 35 metres depth.

02

Triage

Underwater, each fragment is assessed and irreversibly damaged or necrotic tissue is cut away. Removing the dead to save the living — a surgical step that requires a trained eye.

03

Placement of bricks

Terracotta bricks, handmade by a small family company in Tuscany, are positioned independently on the nursery tables. Pre-labeled, inert, porous, and heavy enough to stay in place.

04

Attachment

Each coral fragment is secured to its assigned brick with a specialized resin extensively tested by UNIVPM — chosen over mechanical fixation for soft-coral compatibility.

05

Identification

Every fragment gets a unique code. "1T1.1" means: day 1, Torretta dive site, coral 1, fragment 1. Traceability is the foundation of the monitoring that follows.

04 The Species

What we are trying to save.

Three species sit at the heart of the project — slow-growing, habitat-engineering Mediterranean corals whose populations have been hit hard by heat waves and human activity.

Paramuricea clavata

Violescent sea whip

Large purple-red gorgonian that forms dense colonies on rocky walls. Among the species most severely impacted by Mediterranean marine heat waves.

Eunicella cavolini

Yellow gorgonian

Golden-yellow fan-shaped gorgonian common on Mediterranean rocky substrates, creating three-dimensional habitat for fish and invertebrates.

Corallium rubrum

Red coral

The iconic Mediterranean precious red coral. Grows just a few millimetres per year, making every lost colony a decades-long loss.
